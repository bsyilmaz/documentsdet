1.	Linear	Regression:	The	Design	Matrix	
The	prediction	hypothesis	is	ŷ	=	wᵀx,	where	w	is	the	learnable	weight	vector.	The	
design	matrix	X	(m	×	n)	organizes	the	dataset	with	m	rows	(samples/observations)	
and	n	columns	(features).	Each	row	is	one	data	point;	each	column	is	one	measured	
attribute.	
Key	nuance:	The	normal	equation	w	=	(XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy	gives	the	optimal	weights	in	one	
step	—	no	iteration	needed.	However,	inverting	XᵀX	is	O(n³),	making	it	impractical	
when	n	(features)	is	large.	This	motivates	gradient	descent	for	complex	models.	

2.	Generalization	and	the	i.i.d.	Assumption	
Machine	learning	differs	from	pure	optimization:	optimization	minimizes	training	
error,	while	ML	aims	to	minimize	test	(generalization)	error.	The	theoretical	
guarantee	that	training	performance	transfers	to	test	performance	relies	on	the	i.i.d.	
assumption.	
i.i.d.	=	Independent	and	Identically	Distributed.	Independent	means	knowing	one	
sample	tells	you	nothing	about	another.	Identically	distributed	means	every	sample	
comes	from	the	same	underlying	probability	distribution	p_data.	
Key	nuance:	The	i.i.d.	assumption	is	heavily	violated	in	modern	LLMs	—	text	tokens	
are	correlated,	training	corpora	mix	wildly	different	distributions,	and	deployment	
distributions	differ	from	training.	LLMs	generalize	despite	this	due	to	massive	data	
scale,	implicit	regularization,	and	architectural	inductive	biases.	The	theoretical	
understanding	of	why	this	works	remains	an	active	research	area.	

3.	Capacity	vs.	Error	
Capacity	is	not	simply	parameter	count.	It	refers	to	the	model’s	ability	to	fit	a	wide	
variety	of	functions	—	the	expressiveness	of	the	hypothesis	space.	Contributing	
factors	include:	number	of	parameters,	the	family	of	functions	representable,	and	
architectural	choices	(depth	vs.	width).	
Key	nuance:	Two	models	with	the	same	parameter	count	can	have	very	different	
effective	capacity.	A	deep	narrow	network	has	higher	effective	capacity	than	a	shallow	
wide	one	because	depth	enables	compositional	representations.	The	formal	measure	
is	VC	dimension	(the	largest	number	of	points	a	model	can	shatter),	but	in	practice	
parameter	count	is	used	as	a	rough	proxy.	

 



	

4.	Regularization	and	the	Cost	Function	J(w)	
J(w)	is	the	cost	function	the	optimizer	minimizes.	With	weight	decay:	J(w)	=	
MSE_train	+	λwᵀw.	The	first	term	measures	prediction	error;	the	second	penalizes	
large	weights	(L2	regularization	/	Ridge	regression).	The	term	wᵀw	is	the	dot	product	
of	the	weight	vector	with	itself,	i.e.,	the	sum	of	all	squared	weights.	
λ	(lambda)	is	a	hyperparameter	you	set,	not	something	learned.	When	λ	→	∞,	weights	
are	crushed	to	zero	(underfitting,	flat	line).	When	λ	→	0,	no	penalty	(overfitting,	
wiggly	fit).	Balanced	λ	yields	a	smooth,	generalizable	fit.	
Key	nuance:	Regularization	has	a	deep	Bayesian	interpretation.	L2	regularization	is	
mathematically	equivalent	to	placing	a	Gaussian	prior	on	the	weights.	Saying	“keep	
weights	small”	via	a	penalty	term	is	the	same	as	saying	“I	believe	weights	are	drawn	
from	a	Gaussian	centered	at	zero.”	No	regularization	is	equivalent	to	a	flat/uniform	
prior	—	a	surprisingly	strong	and	often	unreasonable	assumption.	

5.	Point	Estimation:	θ	vs.	θM 	
θ	(theta)	is	the	true	parameter	value	—	fixed	but	unknown.	It	is	the	general	notation	
for	whatever	parameters	your	model	has	(for	linear	regression,	θ	=	w).	θf 	(theta-hat)	is	
the	estimate,	a	function	of	data	and	therefore	a	random	variable.	Different	training	
sets	yield	different	θf 	values.	
Key	nuance:	θ	is	NOT	y.	It	is	the	hidden	truth	about	the	data-generating	process	(e.g.,	
the	true	average	height	of	all	adults	in	Turkey	is	θ).	The	notation	switches	from	w	to	θ	
because	point	estimation	theory	applies	to	any	model,	not	just	linear	regression.	For	a	
Gaussian,	θ	=	{μ,	σ}.	For	a	neural	network,	θ	=	all	millions	of	weights	and	biases.	

6.	Bias	and	Variance:	The	Dart	Board	Analogy	
Bias	=	E[θ> ]	−	θ.	If	you	repeated	the	entire	experiment	infinitely	many	times	and	
averaged	all	θf 	values,	bias	measures	how	far	that	average	lands	from	the	true	θ.	High	
bias	=	systematically	aimed	wrong	(consistent	but	off-target).	Low	bias	=	aimed	at	the	
bullseye	on	average.	
Variance	=	Var(θ> ).	How	much	the	estimate	fluctuates	across	different	datasets.	
Standard	error	SE	=	σ/√m	tells	you	precision	improves	with	the	square	root	of	
sample	size.	Practical	implication:	you	need	to	quadruple	data	to	halve	the	standard	
error	(diminishing	returns).	
Key	nuance:	Bias	is	a	problem	of	wrong	assumptions	—	fix	by	changing	your	model.	
Variance	is	a	problem	of	insufficient	data	or	excess	flexibility	—	fix	with	more	data	or	
regularization.	In	modern	deep	learning,	the	classical	bias-variance	U-curve	is	
violated:	models	with	billions	of	parameters	(far	beyond	the	“overfitting	zone”)	still	
generalize	well,	a	phenomenon	called	“double	descent.”	

7.	MLE	and	MSE:	Two	Sides	of	One	Coin	

 



	

MSE	is	not	an	arbitrary	choice	of	loss	function.	If	you	assume	the	true	y	deviates	from	
your	prediction	by	Gaussian	noise	(p(y|x)	=	N(y;	ŷ(x;w),	σ²)),	then	finding	weights	
that	maximize	the	likelihood	of	the	observed	data	is	mathematically	equivalent	to	
minimizing	MSE.	The	constant	terms	(-m·logσ	and	-(m/2)·log(2π))	vanish	because	
they	don’t	depend	on	w.	
Key	nuance:	Every	common	loss	function	is	MLE	under	a	different	distributional	
assumption.	Gaussian	noise	→	MSE	loss.	Laplacian	noise	→	MAE	(mean	absolute	
error).	Bernoulli	distribution	(classification)	→	cross-entropy	loss.	Understanding	this	
lets	you	derive	the	correct	loss	for	your	problem	rather	than	choosing	from	a	menu.	

8.	Frequentist	vs.	Bayesian	Perspective	
Frequentist:	θ	is	fixed	and	unknown;	θf 	is	random	(depends	on	data).	You	get	one	best	
point	estimate.	Bayesian:	θ	itself	is	uncertain	(a	random	variable);	data	is	fixed	
(observed).	You	get	a	full	distribution	over	θ	via	Bayes’	rule:	p(θ|data)	=	p(data|θ)	×	
p(θ)	/	p(data).	
The	prior	p(θ)	encodes	beliefs	before	seeing	data.	The	likelihood	p(data|θ)	is	the	
evidence	from	data.	The	posterior	p(θ|data)	is	your	updated	belief.	
Key	nuance:	Regularization	and	Bayesian	priors	are	the	same	thing.	L2	regularization	
=	Gaussian	prior	on	weights.	L1	regularization	=	Laplacian	prior.	No	regularization	=	
uniform	prior	(all	weight	values	equally	likely	—	an	unreasonable	assumption).	MLE	
with	regularization	is	secretly	MAP	estimation	(Maximum	A	Posteriori).	Transfer	
learning	also	connects:	a	pretrained	model’s	weights	act	as	a	highly	informative	prior	
for	fine-tuning,	which	is	why	it	works	so	well	in	low-resource	settings.	

9.	Stochastic	Gradient	Descent	and	Minibatches	
For	complex	models	without	closed-form	solutions,	we	optimize	iteratively:	θ	←	θ	−	
ε∇J(θ).	Full	batch	gradient	descent	computes	the	gradient	over	ALL	training	data	
before	each	update	—	accurate	but	slow.	SGD	uses	a	random	minibatch	of	m’	
examples	instead.	
The	minibatch	gradient	is	an	unbiased	estimate	of	the	true	gradient.	Typical	sizes:	32,	
64,	128,	256.	Larger	m’	=	smoother	gradients	but	more	computation	per	step	and	
more	GPU	memory.	Smaller	m’	=	noisier	but	faster	per	step.	
Key	nuance:	The	noise	in	SGD	is	a	feature,	not	a	bug.	Noisy	gradients	help	the	
optimizer	escape	shallow	local	minima	and	find	deeper,	better	valleys	—	like	shaking	
a	ball	on	a	bumpy	surface	so	it	settles	into	the	deepest	hole.	In	LLM	training,	effective	
batch	sizes	are	very	large	(thousands+),	achieved	via	gradient	accumulation	across	
multiple	GPUs,	because	stable	gradients	are	needed	at	scale.	

How	It	All	Connects	

 



	

Chapter	5	builds	a	coherent	chain:	linear	regression	introduces	the	prediction	
framework	and	MSE	loss	→	generalization	theory	explains	why	we	care	about	test	
error	under	i.i.d.	assumptions	→	the	capacity	curve	shows	the	underfitting/overfitting	
tradeoff	→	regularization	controls	capacity	→	point	estimation	formalizes	how	we	
evaluate	estimators	via	bias	and	variance	→	MLE	reveals	that	loss	functions	encode	
distributional	assumptions	→	the	Bayesian	perspective	unifies	regularization	with	
prior	beliefs	→	SGD	provides	the	practical	optimization	engine	for	complex	models.	
Every	concept	in	this	chapter	reappears	in	deep	learning:	loss	functions,	
regularization	(dropout,	weight	decay,	batch	norm),	optimization	(Adam,	learning	
rate	schedules),	and	the	ongoing	mystery	of	why	over-parameterized	networks	
generalize.

1. Softmax & Logits	
Key Formula:  softmax(z)_i = exp(z_i) / Σ_j exp(z_j)	

•  Logits are the raw, unnormalized scores from the final linear layer (before any 
activation). Example: z = [2.0, 1.0, 0.1] → softmax → [0.659, 0.242, 0.099].	

•  All outputs are in range (0, 1) and sum to exactly 1.0.	
•  Numerical stability tip: Never apply softmax manually before PyTorch’s 

nn.CrossEntropyLoss — it applies log-softmax internally to avoid exp() 
overflow.	

2. Cross-Entropy Loss (BCE vs CCE)	
The general form J(θ) = −E[log p_model(y|x)] derives from maximum likelihood 
estimation. It has two practical specializations:	

•  BCE (Binary Cross-Entropy): For 2-class problems. Formula: −[y·log(ŷ) + 
(1−y)·log(1−ŷ)]. Use with sigmoid output.	

•  CCE (Categorical Cross-Entropy): For 3+ class problems. Formula: −Σ 
y_i·log(ŷ_i). Use with softmax output. Only the true class term survives (one-hot y).	

•  Key property: Gradient is large when prediction is wrong → fast learning. No 
saturation problem that MSE has with sigmoid outputs.	
Always pair output activation with the correct loss:	
Sigmoid → BCE  |  Softmax → CCE  |  Linear → MSE	

3. Why Depth Beats Width	
•  Each layer “folds” the input space. After L folds, the network can distinguish 

exponentially many (2^L) regions using only a linear number of parameters.	
•  A shallow-wide network would need exponentially more neurons to match the same 

representational power.	
•  Practical starting point: 2–3 hidden layers. Add more only if underfitting. Widths: 

64, 128, 256, 512 (powers of 2).	
•  Modern LLMs use residual (skip) connections: output = x + sublayer(x). Each 

layer learns a small “delta” correction, not a full rewrite. This enables training 
hundreds of layers without vanishing gradients.	

4. Backpropagation = Chain Rule + Memoization	

 



	

During the forward pass, the network stores intermediate activations a^(k) and 
h^(k) at every layer. These are needed in the backward pass because derivatives 
of nonlinear functions must be evaluated at the original input value.	
Concrete example (ReLU):	
ReLU’(a) = 1 if a > 0, else 0	
a² = 0.3  →  ReLU’(0.3) = 1  (gradient passes through)	
a² = −0.3  →  ReLU’(−0.3) = 0  (dead neuron — gradient killed)	

•  Key efficiency: All gradients computed in O(same time as forward pass) by never 
repeating computations (memoization). PyTorch handles this automatically via 
loss.backward().	

•  Memory implication: Storing activations for all layers is why GPU memory is the 
bottleneck in LLM training. Gradient checkpointing trades recompute time to save 
memory.	

5. Activation Functions	
•  Use ReLU by default in hidden layers. Simple (max(0,x)), avoids vanishing 

gradients when active, computationally cheap.	
•  Use sigmoid/tanh only at output layers or for special architectures (e.g., gates in 

LSTMs).	
•  Dead neuron problem: If a ReLU neuron’s pre-activation is always negative, its 

gradient is always zero — it never learns. Leaky ReLU and ELU are alternatives 
that address this.	

📋 Quick Reference Card	
Logits           → raw scores before activation	
Softmax          → logits to probabilities (multiclass)	
BCE              → 2 classes, sigmoid output	
CCE              → 3+ classes, softmax output	
ReLU             → default hidden activation	
Depth > Width    → exponential regions, linear params	
Backprop         → chain rule + stored activations

1. L2 Regularization (Weight Decay)	
Also known as ridge regression or Tikhonov regularization. L2 regularization adds a penalty 
proportional to the squared magnitude of the weight vector to the loss function.	
Penalty term:	

Ω(θ)	=	½||w||₂²	=	½wᵀw	
The regularized gradient:	

∇𝐰	J0	=	αw	+	∇𝐰	J(w;	X,	y)	
Weight update rule (Eq. 7.5):	

w	←	(1	−	εα)w	−	ε∇𝐰	J(w;	X,	y)	
Derivation:	
Start with the standard gradient descent update w ← w − ε∇J,C  substitute the regularized gradient, 
and distribute ε (the learning rate). The factor (1 − εα) multiplicatively shrinks weights at every step 
before the gradient step is applied. Since εα is a small positive number, this factor is slightly less than 
1, so weights are constantly “decayed” toward zero — hence the name weight decay.	
Geometric interpretation (Figure 7.1):	
The solid ellipses are contours of the unregularized loss J. They are elliptical because the loss is more 
sensitive in some directions than others. The dashed circles are contours of the L2 penalty (all points 
at equal distance from the origin). The Hessian matrix H (second derivatives of J at the optimum w*) 
determines the shape of the ellipses: its eigenvectors define the principal axes, and its eigenvalues λᵢ 
tell you the curvature along each direction.	
The regularized solution w̃ rescales w* along each eigenvector by the factor λᵢ/(λᵢ + α). Directions 
with large eigenvalues (high curvature, the loss cares) are barely affected. Directions with small 
eigenvalues (flat loss surface) are shrunk aggressively toward zero. L2 regularization selectively 
shrinks the weight components that the data does not strongly need.	

2. L1 Regularization (Lasso)	
Penalty term (Eq. 7.18):	

Ω(θ)	=	||w||₁	=	Σᵢ|wᵢ|	
Gradient (sub-gradient, Eq. 7.20):	

 



	

∇𝐰	J0	=	α·sign(w)	+	∇𝐰	J(X,	y;	w)	
Key difference from L2:	
L2 adds αw to the gradient — proportional to the weight. As w gets small, the force pushing it 
toward zero weakens (like a rubber band). L1 adds α·sign(w) — a constant force (always ±α) 
regardless of how small w already is. This constant pressure drives weights all the way to exactly 
zero.	
Sparsity and feature selection:	
A “sparse” model is one where most weight values are literally zero. The analytical solution is: wᵢ = 
sign(wᵢ*) · max{|wᵢ*| − α/Hᵢᵢ, 0}. Each feature has a threshold α/Hᵢᵢ. If the optimal weight |wᵢ*| is 
smaller than this threshold, the feature is completely eliminated (set to zero). If above, it survives but 
is shrunk. This means L1 acts as automatic feature selection: the model itself decides which features 
to keep.	

3. L1 vs L2 Comparison	
L2 (Ridge): Penalty ||w||₂². Shrinks weights toward zero but never exactly to zero. Produces small 
but non-zero weights. Bayesian interpretation: Gaussian prior. Use when all features are relevant.	
L1 (Lasso): Penalty ||w||₁. Drives weights to exactly zero. Produces sparse weights (feature 
selection). Bayesian interpretation: Laplace prior. Use when many features are irrelevant.	
The Bayesian connection:	
Adding a regularization penalty is mathematically equivalent to assuming a prior distribution over 
weights and doing MAP (Maximum A Posteriori) estimation. Taking −log of Bayes’ theorem: −log 
P(w|data) = −log P(data|w) − log P(w). The first term is your loss J, the second is your regularizer. A 
Gaussian prior P(wᵢ) ∝ exp(−wᵢ²/2σ²) yields L2. A Laplace prior P(wᵢ) ∝ exp(−|wᵢ|/b) yields L1. The 
Laplace distribution has a sharp spike at zero (concentrating probability mass at zero), which is why 
L1 produces sparsity.	

4. Norm Penalties as Constrained Optimization	
The penalty approach (minimize J + αΩ) is equivalent to the constraint approach (minimize J subject 
to Ω(θ) ≤ k). The parameter k is a budget — the maximum allowed total weight magnitude. For L2, 
the constraint region is a ball (circle in 2D). For L1, it is a diamond (rotated square in 2D).	
The diamond’s sharp corners sit on the axes (where one coordinate is exactly zero). The loss contours 
expanding from w* are much more likely to first touch the diamond at a corner, producing a sparse 
solution. The L2 ball is smooth, so the contact point typically has all coordinates nonzero.	
In practice, choosing k directly is hard. Instead, we use the penalty approach: add αΩ(w) to the loss 
and tune α via validation. Each α implicitly corresponds to some k, but we never need to know it.	

5. Noise Robustness and Label Smoothing	

 



	

Noise can be added to inputs (equivalent to a weight penalty for small noise), weights (practical 
regularizer for RNNs), or outputs (label smoothing). Label smoothing replaces hard targets (0/1) with 
soft targets: the correct class becomes 1 − ε, each wrong class becomes ε/(k − 1), where k is the 
number of classes.	
This prevents the model from pursuing hard probabilities (exactly 0 or 1), which requires very large 
weights and can never be achieved by a softmax. The typical default is ε = 0.1, popularized by the 
Inception-v2 paper (Szegedy et al., 2016). Practical range: 0.05 for clean labels, 0.1 as default, 0.2 for 
noisy datasets.	

6. Semi-Supervised and Self-Supervised Learning	
Semi-supervised learning uses both labeled (x, y) and unlabeled (x) examples. The core assumption: 
features useful for modeling P(x) are also useful for predicting y. This is the foundation of modern 
pre-training.	
Self-supervised learning is a practical implementation of this idea: it turns unlabeled data into labeled 
data by creating artificial tasks from the data itself (e.g., BERT’s masked language model predicts 
missing words, GPT’s next-token prediction). No human annotation is needed. The modern pipeline 
is: (1) pre-train with self-supervised objectives on massive unlabeled data to learn P(x), then (2) fine-
tune on small labeled data for the actual task to learn P(y|x). Both approaches rest on the same 
fundamental assumption: understanding the structure of data gives transferable representations.	

7. Dropout	
Forward propagation with dropout (3 steps):	
Step 1: Generate a binary mask μ⁽ˡ⁾ ~ Bernoulli(p), where each entry is 1 with probability p and 0 with 
probability (1−p). A fresh mask is generated every training step.	
Step 2: Apply the mask via element-wise multiplication: hC⁽ˡ⁾ = μ⁽ˡ⁾ ⋅ h⁽ˡ⁾. Neurons with mask value 0 are 
“dropped out” and contribute nothing.	
Step 3: Feed masked output into the next layer: h⁽ˡ⁺¹⁾ = f(W⁽ˡ⁺¹⁾ hC⁽ˡ⁾ + b⁽ˡ⁺¹⁾).	
Typical keep probabilities: p = 0.8 for input layers, p = 0.5 for hidden layers. With n neurons, there 
are 2ⁿ possible sub-networks, so dropout effectively trains an exponential ensemble of models that 
share weights.	
Inverted dropout (used by PyTorch and TensorFlow):	
During training, each neuron’s expected output is p ⋅ h (since it is active only p fraction of the time). 
At test time all neurons fire, creating a magnitude mismatch. Inverted dropout solves this during 
training: after masking, divide surviving activations by p. This makes E[output] = p ⋅ (h/p) = h, 
matching the full-network test-time output. No adjustment is needed at inference. Note: PyTorch’s 
nn.Dropout(p) parameter is the drop probability (opposite of the keep probability on the slides).	

8. Adversarial Training	

 



	

FGSM attack (Fast Gradient Sign Method):	
x̃	=	x	+	ε	⋅	sign(∇ₓ	J(θ,	x,	y))	

The perturbation is a function of the loss gradient with respect to the input. It computes which 
direction each pixel should be nudged to maximally increase the loss. The sign function extracts only 
the direction (+1 or −1), and ε controls the perturbation magnitude (small enough to be invisible to 
humans).	
Even tiny per-pixel changes are devastating because in high-dimensional input spaces (e.g., 150,000 
pixels), all perturbations push in the same “wrong” direction constructively. Training on adversarial 
examples forces the model to learn smoother decision boundaries, acting as regularization.	

9. Batch Normalization	
Four steps per mini-batch:	
(1) Compute batch mean: μᴵ = (1/m) Σ xᵢ. (2) Compute batch variance: σ²ᴵ = (1/m) Σ (xᵢ − μᴵ)². (3) 
Normalize: x̂i  = (xᵢ − μᴵ) / √(σ²ᴵ + ε). (4) Scale and shift: yᵢ = γ ⋅ x̂i  + β.	
Why the learnable γ and β?	
Raw normalization (steps 1–3) forces activations to zero mean and unit variance, which is too 
restrictive. For example, sigmoid is approximately linear around zero, so forcing inputs there kills the 
nonlinearity. The learnable parameters γ (scale) and β (shift) let the network learn the optimal 
distribution for each layer. Crucially, if the network learns γ = σᴵ and β = μᴵ, the normalization is 
perfectly undone — so batch norm can be a no-op if that is best. The network is never worse off for 
having batch norm.	
Benefits: reduces internal covariate shift, allows higher learning rates, acts as a regularizer (each 
sample is normalized by noisy batch statistics). At test time, running averages (computed during 
training) replace per-batch statistics.	

Key Takeaways	
• Regularization trades increased bias for reduced variance to improve generalization.	
• L2 shrinks weights proportionally (rubber band); L1 applies constant force (drives to exact zero).	
• Regularizers have Bayesian equivalents: L2 = Gaussian prior, L1 = Laplace prior.	
• Dropout trains exponentially many sub-networks; inverted dropout compensates during training 
for clean inference.	
• Adversarial perturbations exploit the loss gradient w.r.t. inputs; adversarial training regularizes by 
forcing robust decision boundaries.	
• Batch normalization stabilizes training; its learnable γ and β ensure expressiveness is never 
sacrificed for stability.	
• Self-supervised pre-training is the modern realization of semi-supervised learning: learn P(x) first, 
fine-tune for P(y|x).

Deep Learning Optimization & Stability: A Study Guide	
The overarching theme of these concepts is stability. Deep neural networks are fragile. If 
the math isn’t carefully controlled, the learning signal will either die out completely or 
explode into chaos. Everything below is a method to keep the network in the “Goldilocks 
zone” so it can actually learn.	

Part 1: The Signal Flow Problem (Initialization)	
Before a network takes a single learning step, its weights must be assigned starting values. 
If done poorly, the network is paralyzed from step zero.	

The Nuances of Bad Initialization	
• Symmetry Breaking (The Clone Problem): If all weights start at the same number 

(e.g., 0.1), every neuron computes the identical thing, receives the identical 
gradient, and updates identically. The network fails to learn diverse features. 
Nuance: Initialization must be random. 

• Vanishing Gradients (The Whisper): If starting weights are too small, the gradient 
signal shrinks exponentially as it multiplies backward through the layers. Early 
layers hear “nothing” and stop learning. 

• Exploding Gradients (The Megaphone): If starting weights are too large, the 
signal grows exponentially. The math blows up, and updates become wildly 
unstable. 

The Solutions: Variance Matching	
The goal of initialization is to ensure the mathematical variance (the spread of the data) 
going into a layer is perfectly equal to the variance coming out.	

 



	

• Xavier (Glorot) Initialization: Calculates a specific range for random numbers 
based on the number of inputs and outputs. Nuance: Assumes linear-ish 
activations. Best for Sigmoid and Tanh. 

• He (Kaiming) Initialization: The Problem: ReLU activations instantly delete all 
negative numbers, effectively cutting the signal variance in half. Xavier fails here. 
The Fix: He initialization uses the Xavier logic but mathematically multiplies the 
variance by 2 to perfectly compensate for the half of the data ReLU threw away. 

Part 2: The Navigation Problem (Optimizers)	
Once the network is initialized, it has to navigate a complex, multi-dimensional landscape 
(the loss surface) to find the lowest possible error.	

1. Vanilla Gradient Descent (GD) & Momentum	
• The Problem: On loss surfaces shaped like narrow canyons (“poor conditioning”), 

standard GD bounces violently back and forth across the steep canyon walls and 
makes agonizingly slow progress down the actual valley floor. 

• Momentum: Adds “velocity” to the update. It remembers the previous steps. 
Nuance: The side-to-side bouncing forces cancel each other out, while the tiny 
pushes forward accumulate, eventually driving the optimizer up to “terminal 
velocity” straight down the valley. 

• Nesterov Momentum (NAG): Nuance: Standard momentum blindly builds speed 
and often overshoots the bottom of the hill. Nesterov does a “ghost jump” based 
on its current velocity, calculates the gradient in the future, and uses that to hit 
the brakes before overshooting. 

2. Adaptive Learning Rates (AdaGrad, RMSProp, Adam)	
Standard optimizers use one global learning rate (ε). This forces a compromise: too high, 
and frequent features (common words) become unstable; too low, and rare features (rare 
words) never learn.	

• AdaGrad: Gives every single parameter its own learning rate. It divides the global 
rate by the square root of a historical accumulator of all past gradients (r). The 
Fatal Flaw: Because it permanently accumulates every gradient, the denominator 
eventually gets so massive that the learning rate shrinks to exactly 0, and training 
freezes prematurely. 

• RMSProp: Fixes AdaGrad’s flaw. Nuance: Instead of keeping a permanent history, 
it uses an Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (a “leaky bucket”). It 
intentionally forgets older gradients so the learning rate adapts dynamically to the 
current landscape without shrinking to zero. 

 



	

• Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation): The industry standard. It combines the 
best of both worlds. Nuance: It tracks the 1st moment (Momentum, for speed and 
direction) and the 2nd moment (RMSProp, for smart, per-parameter braking). It 
also includes a “bias correction” to prevent the math from artificially lagging at 
the very beginning of training. 

Part 3: The Shifting Target Problem (Normalization)	
Even with perfect initialization and Adam, deep networks suffer from Internal Covariate 
Shift. As layer 1 updates its weights, it completely changes the mathematical distribution 
of the data it hands to layer 2. Every layer is constantly trying to learn a moving target.	

Batch Normalization	
Acts as a quality control checkpoint between layers, forcing the data back into a stable 
distribution (mean 0, variance 1) for the current mini-batch.	

• The Nuance of γ and β: Forcing data to mean 0, variance 1 can sometimes destroy 
the network’s expressive power (e.g., squashing data only into the linear part of a 
Sigmoid curve). So, Batch Norm introduces learnable parameters γ (scale) and β 
(shift). This allows the network to mathematically “undo” the normalization if it 
decides a different distribution is actually optimal for that specific layer. 

Layer Norm vs. Batch Norm	
• Batch Norm calculates statistics across the batch dimension. Flaw: It breaks if 

your batch size is 1, and it struggles heavily with variable-length text sequences 
(RNNs/Transformers). 

• Layer Norm calculates statistics across the features of a single sample 
independently. Nuance: Because it doesn’t rely on the batch at all, it is the 
absolute gold standard for NLP and Transformer architectures (like BERT and 
GPT).
